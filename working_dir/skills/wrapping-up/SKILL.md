# Wrapping Up

## What this is for

You made something. Now let's save it so it's safe, take a moment to enjoy the win, and figure out what the person wants to do next. This is the happy ending of the journey — keep it warm and celebratory.

**Say at the start:** "Let's save your work and look at what we made."

## How to wrap up

### 1. Celebrate first
Before anything technical, name what they accomplished. "You just built a real, working [thing] from scratch. That's genuinely cool." Be specific about what they made — it makes the win feel real.

### 2. Save the work
Make sure their work is safely saved. In plain language: "I'm going to save a snapshot of everything we made, so it's safe and we can always come back to it." Then save it (commit the work) with a clear, friendly message describing what was built.

If saving their work involves anything that can't be easily undone — sharing it publicly, putting it online, deleting anything — pause and explain it first, then let them decide. Never do something hard to reverse without checking.

### 3. Show them where things are
Tell the person, in plain words, where their work lives and how to find it again. "Everything is in this folder. The main page is the file called `index.html` — that's the one you open in a browser."

### 4. Offer what's next
Give them a few friendly, concrete options. Keep it short and let them choose:

> "A few things we could do from here:
> 1. Add one of the features we saved for later
> 2. Change how it looks or feels
> 3. Put it online so others can see it
> 4. Stop here for now — it's in great shape
>
> What sounds good?"

Match the options to what makes sense for their project. Don't overwhelm — three or four is plenty.

## Keep it jargon-free

When you save the work, you're using version control underneath, but the person doesn't need to know those words yet. "Saving a snapshot" is enough. If they're curious and ask, explain gently — but don't lead with it.

## If they want to put it online

If the person wants to share their thing with the world, that's a real milestone — treat it as exciting, and walk them through it one calm step at a time. Explain that putting something online means anyone with the link can see it, so they know what they're choosing. Pick the simplest option that fits, and explain each step as you go.

## Remember

- Celebrate before you do anything technical.
- Save the work so it's safe.
- Explain anything that can't be undone before doing it.
- Tell them where their work lives in plain words.
- Offer a few clear options for what's next, and let them choose.
